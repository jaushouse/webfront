Automation tools promise to save you hours every week by connecting your apps and eliminating repetitive tasks. And they can — when used right. But they can also become expensive, confusing time sinks if you pick the wrong tool or automate the wrong things.

Let's break down the three big names — Zapier, Make (formerly Integromat), and n8n — and figure out which one, if any, makes sense for your business.

## First: What Do These Tools Actually Do?

All three do the same basic thing: they connect your apps together so that when something happens in one place, something else happens automatically in another.

Examples:
- A new form submission in your website automatically creates a contact in your CRM
- When you mark an invoice as paid in QuickBooks, a notification goes to Slack
- New email subscribers get added to your email marketing tool and tagged based on which form they filled out
- When a deal closes in your CRM, a project gets created in your project management tool

Think of them as the plumbing between your business apps. Each one approaches it differently, though.

## Zapier: The Easy One

Zapier is the most popular automation tool, and it's popular because it's the easiest to use. If you've never built an automation before, Zapier is where you start.

**Pricing:**
- Free: 100 tasks/month, 5 single-step Zaps
- Starter: ~$20/month (750 tasks)
- Professional: ~$49/month (2,000 tasks)
- Team: ~$69/month/user (shared workspace)
- Enterprise: custom

**What Zapier does well:**
- Easiest to learn — you can build your first automation in 10 minutes
- Largest app library (7,000+ integrations)
- Great documentation and templates
- "Zaps" are simple to understand: "When THIS happens, do THAT"
- AI features for building Zaps from plain English descriptions
- Reliable — it just works for most common use cases

**Where Zapier falls short:**
- Most expensive of the three, especially as task volume grows
- Complex workflows with branching logic get clunky
- Single-step Zaps on the free plan are very limiting
- "Tasks" add up fast — each step in a multi-step Zap counts as a task
- Less control over error handling and data transformation

**Best for:** Non-technical users who want to set up simple automations quickly. If you need to connect two or three apps with straightforward logic, Zapier is the fastest path.

## Make: The Visual One

Make (which used to be called Integromat) takes a visual approach to automation. Instead of simple "if this, then that" chains, you build flowchart-style scenarios with modules and connections. It's more powerful than Zapier but requires more thought.

**Pricing:**
- Free: 1,000 operations/month, 2 active scenarios
- Core: ~$9/month (10,000 operations)
- Pro: ~$16/month (10,000 operations + advanced features)
- Teams: ~$29/month/user
- Enterprise: custom

**What Make does well:**
- Visual workflow builder that makes complex automations understandable
- Much cheaper than Zapier for the same volume of work
- Better handling of complex logic — branching, filters, loops, error handling
- "Operations" are counted more fairly than Zapier's "tasks"
- Data transformation and manipulation is more powerful
- Good API integration for custom connections

**Where Make falls short:**
- Steeper learning curve than Zapier
- Fewer pre-built integrations (though still 1,500+, which covers most needs)
- The visual builder can get messy with very complex workflows
- Debugging can be confusing for beginners
- Documentation isn't as polished as Zapier's

**Best for:** People who are comfortable learning a tool and need more complex automations. If you're connecting more than two apps or need conditional logic (do X if this, do Y if that), Make handles it better and cheaper than Zapier.

## n8n: The Technical One

n8n is the open-source option. You can self-host it for free or use their cloud version for a fee. It's the most powerful of the three, but it's also the most technical.

**Pricing:**
- Self-hosted: free (you provide the server)
- Cloud Starter: ~$20/month (2,500 executions)
- Cloud Pro: ~$50/month (10,000 executions)
- Enterprise: custom

**What n8n does well:**
- Self-hosting means your data stays on your servers (important for some industries)
- Most powerful workflow capabilities — you can build almost anything
- No per-operation limits on self-hosted (just your server's capacity)
- Code nodes let you write custom JavaScript when needed
- Active open-source community
- Fair pricing on cloud plans

**Where n8n falls short:**
- Self-hosting requires technical knowledge (server setup, maintenance, updates)
- Steepest learning curve of the three
- Smallest library of pre-built integrations
- The interface is functional but not as polished
- If something breaks on self-hosted, you're the support team
- Fewer tutorials and resources available

**Best for:** Businesses with technical staff (or a technical partner) who need maximum flexibility and control. If data privacy is a concern and you want to keep everything on your own servers, n8n is the clear choice. But be honest about your technical capabilities.

## The Real Comparison

**Ease of use:** Zapier >>> Make > n8n. Not close. If nobody on your team is technical, Zapier is the only realistic option.

**Price for volume:** n8n (self-hosted) > Make > n8n (cloud) > Zapier. Zapier's pricing is the most painful at scale.

**Power and flexibility:** n8n > Make > Zapier. Inverse of ease of use, which is exactly what you'd expect.

**Number of integrations:** Zapier > Make > n8n. Zapier connects to everything. If your tool isn't in Make or n8n's library, you might need to build a custom integration.

**Reliability:** All three are reliable for standard use cases. Zapier has the edge for simple automations. Make and n8n give you more control over error handling for complex ones.

## When You Don't Need Any of Them

This is the part most comparison articles skip. You might not need an automation tool if:

**Your apps already talk to each other.** Many modern tools have built-in integrations. Your CRM might already connect to your email marketing tool directly. Check before you add middleware.

**You're automating something that happens twice a week.** If a task takes 2 minutes and happens a few times a week, the time spent building and maintaining the automation might never pay off. Do the math.

**The process isn't stable yet.** Don't automate a workflow that changes every month. Get the process right first, then automate it. Automating a bad process just makes it break faster.

**You're creating complexity to avoid one simple task.** We've seen people build 10-step automations to avoid a 30-second copy-paste. Sometimes the manual way is fine.

## How to Decide

Here's the decision tree we'd use:

1. **Have you identified a specific, repetitive task that connects two or more apps?** If no, you don't need an automation tool yet. Go find those tasks first.

2. **Is the automation simple (two apps, straightforward logic)?** Start with Zapier. You'll be up and running in minutes.

3. **Do you need complex logic, branching, or high volume?** Look at Make. Better price, more power, moderate learning curve.

4. **Do you have technical staff and need maximum control or data privacy?** Consider n8n self-hosted. But only if you can maintain it.

5. **Are you doing fewer than 100 automations per month?** The free plans might be all you need. Don't pay until you have to.

## Our Honest Take

For most small businesses, Zapier is the right starting point. Yes, it's the most expensive at scale. But the time you save by using the easiest tool usually outweighs the cost difference, especially when you're just getting started with automation.

If you find yourself bumping up against Zapier's limits — too many tasks, too expensive, need more complex logic — that's when Make becomes worth the switch.

n8n is excellent, but it's an honest recommendation only if you have someone technical who can own it. Otherwise, you're trading one problem (manual tasks) for another (maintaining infrastructure).

Start small. Automate one thing. See the value. Then expand.
