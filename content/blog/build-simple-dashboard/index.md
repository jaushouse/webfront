You know that feeling when someone asks "how's business?" and your answer is basically a gut feeling? Revenue feels good. We seem busy. I think we're doing better than last quarter.

That's not a dashboard. That's a guess.

A dashboard gives you actual numbers — the handful of metrics that tell you how your business is really doing, updated regularly, in one place. And building one doesn't require expensive software, a data analyst, or an engineering degree.

Here's how to build a dashboard that's simple enough to maintain and useful enough to check every day.

## Why Most Small Business Dashboards Fail

Before we build one, let's talk about why most attempts don't stick.

**Too many metrics.** Someone reads an article about KPIs and decides they need to track 47 things. Three weeks later, nobody's updating the dashboard because it takes two hours.

**Too complicated to update.** If the dashboard requires pulling data from six different sources and manually entering it every morning, it won't survive past the first week.

**No connection to decisions.** A metric that doesn't lead to action is just a number. If you see it and shrug, it shouldn't be on your dashboard.

**Designed for someone else.** Dashboards built for investors, banks, or partners look impressive but often track things that don't help you run your business day-to-day.

The goal is a dashboard that takes less than five minutes to check and tells you something useful every time you look at it.

## Step 1: Pick 5-7 Metrics That Actually Matter

Here's the hard part — choosing what to track. The temptation is to track everything. Resist it.

For most small businesses in the 5-50 employee range, these categories cover what matters:

### Money In
- **Revenue this month vs. last month (and vs. same month last year)** — are you growing?
- **Outstanding invoices (accounts receivable)** — how much money is owed to you and how old is it?
- **Cash in the bank** — the most important number for any small business

### Money Out
- **Total expenses this month** — are you spending more than expected?
- **Biggest expense categories** — where is the money going?

### Customers
- **New customers this month** — is your pipeline healthy?
- **Customer churn** (if applicable) — are you losing people?

### Work
- **Jobs/projects in progress** — how full is your plate?
- **Jobs completed this month** — are you delivering?
- **Average time to completion** — are you getting faster or slower?

### Team
- **Utilization rate** (billable hours / available hours) — is your team busy with the right work?

Pick five to seven from this list. Don't pick all of them. Choose the ones where a change in the number would make you do something differently.

Here's a good test: if a metric drops by 25%, would you take action? If yes, it belongs on your dashboard. If you'd just say "huh, interesting" and move on, leave it off.

## Step 2: Figure Out Where the Data Lives

For each metric you picked, write down:

- Where does this data currently exist? (QuickBooks, your CRM, a spreadsheet, your head)
- How often is it updated? (Real-time, daily, weekly, monthly)
- How hard is it to get the number? (One click, a report, a manual calculation)

This tells you how easy or hard your dashboard will be to build. If all your metrics are in QuickBooks and your CRM, you're in good shape. If some of them require calling your accountant and waiting three days, you have a data collection problem to solve first.

## Step 3: Choose Your Dashboard Tool

You have three main options, ranging from simple to sophisticated:

### Option A: A Spreadsheet (Google Sheets or Excel)
**Best for:** Businesses just getting started with dashboards, or those with fewer than 10 employees.

Build a single sheet with your metrics, updated weekly or daily. Use conditional formatting to highlight numbers that need attention (red for bad, green for good). Create a simple chart or two for trends.

**Pros:** Free, flexible, everyone knows how to use it.
**Cons:** Manual updates required, no automatic data pulling (unless you get fancy with add-ons).

### Option B: A Dashboard Tool (Databox, Geckoboard, Klipfolio)
**Best for:** Businesses that want automatic updates and a polished display.

These tools connect directly to your software (QuickBooks, HubSpot, Google Analytics, etc.) and pull data automatically. You build your dashboard by dragging and dropping widgets.

**Pros:** Automatic updates, looks professional, can display on a TV in the office.
**Cons:** Monthly subscription ($50-200/month), limited by available integrations.

### Option C: Built-in Reporting
**Best for:** Businesses that use an all-in-one tool like Odoo, or that have most of their data in one system.

Many business tools have built-in dashboards and reporting. QuickBooks has financial dashboards. HubSpot has sales dashboards. If most of your metrics come from one tool, the built-in reporting might be all you need.

**Pros:** No extra cost, data is always up to date, no setup required.
**Cons:** Limited to data within that one tool, can't combine data from multiple sources.

For most small businesses, **Option A (spreadsheet) is the right starting point.** You can always upgrade later. The important thing is to start tracking, not to have the prettiest tool.

## Step 4: Build It (The Spreadsheet Version)

Here's how to build a simple but effective dashboard in Google Sheets:

**Create a "Dashboard" tab.** This is what you'll look at. Keep it clean — no clutter.

**Layout your metrics in a grid:**

| Metric | This Week | Last Week | Change | Target |
|--------|-----------|-----------|--------|--------|
| Revenue | $12,400 | $11,200 | +10.7% | $12,000 |
| Cash in bank | $45,600 | $43,200 | +5.6% | $40,000 |
| Outstanding invoices | $8,300 | $9,100 | -8.8% | < $10,000 |
| New customers | 3 | 2 | +1 | 3 |
| Jobs in progress | 7 | 8 | -1 | — |
| Jobs completed | 4 | 3 | +1 | 4 |

**Add conditional formatting:**
- Green when you're at or above target
- Yellow when you're within 10% of target
- Red when you're below target

**Add a trend chart.** Pick your top two metrics (probably revenue and cash) and add a simple line chart showing the last 12 weeks. Trends are more useful than snapshots.

**Create a "Data Entry" tab.** This is where you input raw numbers each week. Keep it separate from the dashboard so the dashboard stays clean.

## Step 5: Make It a Habit

A dashboard is only useful if you look at it. Build it into your routine:

**Monday morning check.** Spend five minutes reviewing the dashboard at the start of every week. What changed? What needs attention? This sets your priorities for the week.

**Update schedule.** Block 15 minutes every Friday (or Monday morning) to update the numbers. If you skip this, the dashboard becomes stale and useless.

**Team visibility.** Share the dashboard with your leadership team or put it on a screen in the office. When everyone can see the numbers, accountability happens naturally.

**Monthly review.** Once a month, spend 30 minutes looking at trends. Are you moving in the right direction? Are there patterns? Monthly data tells a different story than weekly data.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**Dashboard overload.** If you can't take in your entire dashboard in 30 seconds, it has too much on it. Cut metrics until it fits on one screen without scrolling.

**Vanity metrics.** Social media followers, website visits, or email list size might feel good but they rarely connect to revenue or operations. Track them somewhere else if you want, but keep them off your main dashboard.

**No targets.** Numbers without context are meaningless. "$12,000 in revenue this week" — is that good or bad? Add targets based on your budget, last year's performance, or your growth goals. Then the number tells a story.

**Tracking inputs instead of outcomes.** "Number of sales calls made" is an input. "New customers acquired" is an outcome. Outcomes belong on your dashboard. Inputs belong in team management discussions.

**Analysis paralysis.** Don't spend more time analyzing the dashboard than acting on what it tells you. If revenue is down, the answer isn't "let me look at 15 more reports." The answer is "let me talk to my sales team."

## Growing Your Dashboard Over Time

Start simple. Five metrics on a spreadsheet. Get in the habit of checking it. After a month, you'll naturally know whether you need more detail, different metrics, or a better tool.

When you're ready to level up:

- Move to an automated dashboard tool that pulls data directly from your software
- Add a second dashboard for a specific area (like sales or operations)
- Set up email alerts for metrics that drop below a threshold

But don't rush this. A simple dashboard you actually use beats a sophisticated one that collects dust.
